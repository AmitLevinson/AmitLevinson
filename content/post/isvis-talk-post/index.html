Community

Concurrent to wanting to learn R I also started roaming across Twitter. One day I came across the #TidyTuesday challenge, a data visualization challenge oriented to the R community. every week a new dataset is published and the R community is invited to analyze and visualize it.

Observing for a few weeks I decided to participate in week 39, 2020. Doing a quick EDA I noticed a trend, somewhat intuitive, of an upward increase in the number of visitors to parks across years. Remembering a plot with added icons from the week before I adapted some of the code and produced my first plot. I posted it on Twitter and the feedback was amazing.

As time went by I joined additional projects such as the #30DayMapChallenge and #30DayChart challenge. In different from the #TidyTuesday, here you bring your own data. Additionally, the challenges occur through a whole month, where each day has its own theme and participants are invited to share maps/charts relating to that theme.

While the two challenges differ, I found a lot in common: Anyone and everyone are invited, beginners and advanced alike. Graph it in R, Python, Tableau, Qgis and whatnot. But most importantnly despite not knowing almost anyone I felt like I was part of a community.

Learning

Learning from others

One of the things I love about these challenges is that many of the participants share their code, which you can then adapt for your own needs.

During the #30DayMapChallenge in 2019 Alexandra Kapp published a map of frequently traveled routes in Berlin. What she did was, to my interpertation, elegantly brilliant: She sampled two points in Berlin and calculated the fastest route between them. She did this for 500 pairs of points and plotted the routes traveled on with the line width representing the frequency of the street traveled compared to other streets.

Seeing this and amazed by its simplictiy I adapted it for my own needs: The next year I ran the same analysis on Tel-Aviv, producing the plot on the right.

While this was one example, adapting others‚Äô code is something prevalent in a coder‚Äôs life: You find an answer on StackOverflow, copy the example in a GitHub repo documentation and more. Participating in these challenges de-facto motivated me to learn others‚Äô code, way of thinking and different approaches to the same data.

What I love about these challenges is even without deliberatly attempting to challenge ourselves it occurs: The data is slightly different than what we‚Äôre used to, we try out one new function and other things that expand our knowledge one step further.

Learning things I implement at work

By the time I started working at Payoneer I participated in various challenges and had the opportunity to participate in a few more since.

For example the whole idea of pre-attentive attention, how do I guide my reader through the chart, showing him where to focus first, second and last. At other times it was more specific things such as annotations, and using them to guide the audience to the main takeaway at hand.
